I love how the reading, lecture, as well as the video, were all related in some way. I particularly enjoyed the video about bad doors (It’s not you. Bad doors are everywhere 2016) as it explored the importance of design decisions and the general interface and interaction with things. Don Norman speaks about how discoverability and feedback are important in the design of things. This really struck a cord with me and it’s something that I will continue to think about whenever I make something. Don says, “why do you have to have a sign that says push or pull? Why not make it obvious?”. This point ties into one from the reading, “traditional conventions are repetitions that become regularities against which differences stand out” (Mailloux 2015: 400). When practices are repeated it becomes a convention that is understood by the majority of people. For example, the colour red has always been made to signify danger. Through the repeated use of this colour to represent danger, it is now a convention for representing danger, and just like Don said, there is now no need to point out what it means, it is obvious. This can also tie in with web design since there are already certain conventions and repeated practices which make it easier for people to understand. If I place a button or link on my website, having it highlighted when hovered over or when clicked on is a repeated practice and a convention for good usability and a good interface. It makes the action obvious and it gives the user feedback. Since web design is all about the user interface and experience, it is important to know how to design an interface that will give off a good experience.

There are so many things to learn within all these coding languages, and the more you interact with it and the more content you absorb the more well versed you will become with it.

Reflection on readings: The first article, "Digital Hegemonies: The Localness of Search Engine Results" by Ballatore, Graham, and Sen (2017), focuses specifically on search engine results and how they can vary depending on location. It discusses how these variations can create biases and power dynamics in information access. The second article, "Towards a Study of Information Geographies: (Im)Mutable Augmentations and a Mapping of the Geographies of Information" by Graham, De Sabbata, and Zook (2015), takes a broader approach by exploring the concept of information geographies. It examines how information is distributed unevenly across the globe and how factors like power, politics, and infrastructure shape these geographies. Both articles highlight the importance of understanding the complex relationship between digital technologies, geography, and the access and distribution of information. They raise awareness about the potential biases and inequalities that can arise in the digital realm, emphasizing the need for further research and analysis to address these issues.

After going through all of these readings you begin to see that websites and the internet as a whole are not just made up of code, but there's so much more to it. Businesses and corporations often have ulterior motives and agendas. As South African web developers it is our duty to ensure that the work we do and the websites we make are a means of goodness for the people that access it and we should try to increase the representation of South African work. Web design is not only based on making a functioning website that looks good, but there are many responsibilities tied to it as well.
